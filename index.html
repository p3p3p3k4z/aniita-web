About Añi Ita

Harvesting food, cultivating life.

The Añi Ita project (Soul of the Flower, in Mixtec) is the master plan for creating a national network of Active Ecological Infrastructure. The "Pollinator Islands" are not just patches of flowers; they are strategically designed bioproductivity engines integrated into productive landscapes, adapted to the region to generate a virtuous cycle.

Furthermore, Añi Ita integrates data science, ecological knowledge, and accessible technology to support the large-scale implementation of these solutions.

The Problem

We face a brutal paradox: to feed a growing population, industrial agriculture has drained the land of life. Each hectare of monoculture, though productive in the short term, becomes a desert for pollinators, unbalances the ecosystem, degrades the soil, and makes us dependent on chemical inputs.

This dependency is a strategy that leads us toward food insecurity and ecological collapse, sacrificing long-term resilience for short-term efficiency. The solution is not to stop agriculture but to reinvent it by restoring fundamental ecological interactions.

Main Flora of the Country

Dahlia campanulata

This flower is native to the states of Oaxaca and Guerrero. Its distinctive bell shape and petals that curve outwards and downwards make it unique within the dahlia family. It generally blooms from summer to early autumn, bringing color and life to the spaces where it is found.

Salvia mexicana

Mexican sage, known for its showy flowering and ability to attract pollinators, is a ruderal plant favored by disturbance, so it is common to find it on roadsides or cultivated fields. It is natively distributed from the northwest to the south of Mexico, in mixed forests and grasslands.

Flor de ocelote

The Tigridia pavonia plant, also known as Oceloxóchitl in Nahuatl, which means "ocelot flower" or "tiger flower," is prized for its showy, vibrantly colored flowers that, despite lasting only one day, feature spots or tiger-like patterns inside, from which it gets its name.

Cempasúchil

The cempasúchil or flower of the dead, used in the Day of the Dead celebrations, is an iconic flower of Mexico. It is believed that its vibrant color and aroma attract pollinators like butterflies and bees. Pollination is crucial for seed production, ensuring its proliferation each season.

Magnolia pacífica

The Pacific Magnolia is a tree native to Mexico, growing in Sinaloa, Nayarit, and Jalisco. Its wood is used in carpentry, and its flower has uses in traditional medicine to treat heart problems. It is endangered due to habitat loss from deforestation and land-use change.

Ahuehuete

Also known as sabino or Mexican cypress, it is one of the most emblematic trees in Mexico. Its name in Nahuatl means "old man of the water," as it grows near rivers, springs, and bodies of water. Today it is protected for its ecological and cultural value.

Mexico's Pollinators: Nature's Little Heroes

Pollinators are essential for plant reproduction, as they transport pollen between flowers, allowing new fruits and seeds to grow. It is estimated that 90% of flowering plants depend on them.

Bees

Bees, in all their forms, provide us with products like honey, pollen, and wax. They are fundamental to our diet.

Stingless Bees

These bees, like Scaptotrigona mexicana, use chemical and thermal signals to call their companions to new food sources.

Butterflies

They are mainly active during the day and help pollinate many plants, contributing to the biodiversity of the flora.

Moths

Although some species, like the black witch moth, are associated with myths related to death, their true role is to pollinate night-blooming plants, being essential for the balance of many ecosystems.

Bats

Agave-visiting bats, which can live up to 12 years, are key pollinators, especially for nocturnal plants.

Hummingbirds

These tiny, agile birds not only fly forwards and backward but also consume their weight in nectar every day, contributing to the pollination of many flowers.
